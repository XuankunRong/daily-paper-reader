Abstract 

This article establishes the foundational mathematical limits of Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) safety, proving that the core barrier is not the impossibility of an aligned state, but its structural unverifiability. We formalize this boundary through two central impossibility results: the Unverifiability Theorem of Alignment and the Theorem of Finite Structural Unverifiabil-ity of AGI Alignment. We ground this boundary at Trakhtenbrot’s Wall, demonstrating that contemporary engineering defenses relying on finite hardware or halting architectures fail to es-cape logical obstructions. This failure manifests as an inescapable triad of containment failures: open domains yield fundamental undecidability (Rice and Gödel); universal finite verification collapses into algorithmic incomputability (Trakhtenbrot); and particular bounded environments trap the supervisor within intractable bounds in the worst case. As a direct structural corollary of these results, we derive the Soundness–Completeness–Tractability Trilemma, establishing that the mutual incompatibility of these three properties is a necessary consequence of descriptive complexity rather than an empirical anomaly. Finally, we map these theoretical bounds onto practical AI engineering, demonstrating that modern containment strategies are not temporary patches, but mandatory sacrifices of logical expressivity required to secure decidable fragments of safety. 

Keywords: Artificial General Intelligence (AGI), AI Alignment, Computability Theory, De-scriptive Complexity, Logical Expressivity, Trakhtenbrot’s Theorem, Gödel’s Incompleteness, Rice’s Theorem, Formal Verification.

# 2 The Unverifiability of AGI Alignment: The Fallacy of Universal Finite Verification 

The alignment of Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) poses the challenge of ensuring that an au-tonomous system, possessing unbounded learning and recursive capabilities, operates exclusively within human-specified safety constraints. The underlying mathematical problem is to determine whether an absolute verifier can be constructed for this purpose. While current machine learning safety paradigms rely heavily on empirical benchmarks and heuristic containment, they lack a foun-dational theory of what is formally computable. This paper addresses this gap by establishing the fundamental limits of formal verification applied to recursive architectures. Crucially, this analysis grounds alignment within the inescapable tension between logical expressivity and computational tractability. This tension mirrors the classical friction between syntax and semantics: universal dis-crete recursion inevitably triggers either logical self-reference or combinatorial explosion. Through the lens of descriptive complexity, we demonstrate that absolute verification is not an engineering optimization problem, but a structural impossibility bounded by the exact undecidability thresholds of mathematical logic. Contemporary AI safety strategies often rely on a framework we denote as Universal Finite Verification . This paradigm posits that the safety of an AGI agent can be guaranteed by confining its operation to a bounded, finite-state environment where all possible behaviors are, in principle, verifiable. However, as this article demonstrates, this reliance on finite-state confinement is funda-mentally flawed. By grounding our analysis in Trakhtenbrot’s theorem, we show that even within strictly finite domains, the verification of sufficiently expressive agents collapses into algorithmic incomputability, rendering global AGI verification computationally infeasible. While Trakhtenbrot’s theorem is a foundational result in finite model theory, its implications for the safety of General Artificial Intelligence have remained largely unexplored. This article bridges the gap between classical descriptive complexity and modern AI safety, formally demonstrat-ing that Trakhtenbrot’s classic undecidability results constitute a structural ‘wall’—a fundamental constraint—that renders universal AGI verification not merely difficult, but logically intractable within the framework of formal containment. 

Theorem 1 (Unverifiability Theorem of Alignment) . There is no universal algorithmic procedure capable of certifying the safe behavior of a highly expressive AGI infallibly, completely, and tractably.

Thesis Statement We argue that the absolute verification of AGI alignment is not merely an engineering hurdle susceptible to incremental hardware or software optimization, but a structural barrier of formal unverifiability. Any universal verification procedure is fundamentally bounded by insurmountable logical and computational barriers. We demonstrate that this constraint is exhaustive, effectively closing the only two possible computational vectors available to an external verifier: 1. Section 7.1 provides the formal argument that these two vectors are jointly exhaustive, closing the only possible gap in this dichotomy. 

# 3 The Threshold of Computational Universality and the Paradox of Difficulty 

We define the difference between Narrow AI and AGI in terms of logical expressivity, which reveals a profound paradox regarding computational difficulty. 

• Narrow AI: The model Mnarrow operates over continuous or decidable logical structures. For instance, systems based on Real Closed Fields (RCF), which describe continuous spaces, are complete and decidable (Tarski, 1951) precisely because their logic is too “simple” to define the structure of natural numbers. Since they cannot represent discrete structures, self-referential paradoxes do not arise. Here, tractable universal certification is possible. 

• AGI: To achieve general intelligence, the architecture Magi must be capable of expressing general computable functions, requiring a discrete structure. Mathematically, it contains Peano Arithmetic ( PA = ⟨N, +, ×, 0, 1, s ⟩). It is precisely this discrete nature that allows Gödel numbering and enables self-reference. The moment the system accepts discrete iterated multiplication, it enters the class of Turing-complete languages, where intelligence acquires its power but pays the price of undecidability. 

# 4 The Semantic Barrier: Proof via Rice’s Theorem 

We now establish the first impossibility result, operating over open (infinite) domains. The argument follows and sharpens the proof given by Melo et al. (2025). 

Setup and notation Let an AGI be formalised as a Turing machine Mx, where x ∈ N is a Gödel index. Write φx for the partial computable function computed by Mx. An alignment property is a 3set P of partial computable functions. We say that P is non-trivial if it is neither empty nor the set of all partial computable functions — equivalently, there exist a function f ∈ P (a ‘safe’ behaviour) and a function g / ∈ P (an ‘unsafe’ behaviour). The corresponding index set is: 

IP = {x ∈ N | φx ∈ P }

A universal alignment verifier would be an algorithm that decides, given an arbitrary index x,whether φx ∈ P . Theorem 2 shows no such algorithm can exist. 

Theorem 2 (Rice, 1953) . Let P be a non-trivial property of partial computable functions. Then IP

is undecidable. Proof (many-one reduction from the Halting Problem). Let HP = {⟨ x, w ⟩ | Mx halts on input w}

denote the Halting Problem, which is known to be undecidable (Turing, 1936). We reduce HP to 

IP , showing that any decision procedure for IP would yield one for HP , a contradiction. Since P is non-trivial, fix a function q ∈ P and note that the nowhere-defined function f∅ (the function that diverges on every input) satisfies f∅ /∈ P (if f∅ ∈ P one replaces q and f∅ in the argument below). Given any pair ⟨x, w ⟩, construct the machine M ′ that on input y:1. simulates Mx on w;2. if and when that simulation halts, computes and returns q(y).By construction:

Hence M ′ ∈ IP ⇐⇒ ⟨x, w ⟩ ∈ HP . A decision procedure for IP would therefore decide HP .Since HP is undecidable, no such procedure exists. 

Corollary 1 (Semantic inviability of alignment over open domains) . Evaluating any non-trivial alignment property of a Turing-complete AGI is undecidable. In particular, no effective verifier can certify safe behaviour over the full input domain. 

Note on scope: Rice’s Theorem is a statement about semantic properties — those determined solely by the input/output behaviour of Mx, independently of its internal description. Structural properties (e.g., ‘the description of Mx has at most 100 states’) are decidable and fall outside the theorem. For alignment, however, the relevant properties are precisely semantic: safety is about what the system does , not about its source code. 

# 5 The Expressivity Barrier: From Completeness to Gödel’s Incom-pleteness 

The undecidability established by Rice operates dynamically : it concerns the behaviour of a running machine over all inputs. One might attempt to sidestep this by adopting a static approach: fix a formal framework F prior to execution and prove F ⊢ φsafe . This section shows that the choice of 

F itself leads to an inescapable dilemma between descriptive adequacy and deductive completeness — a collapse whose precise form is Gödel’s Incompleteness. 

The designer’s dilemma. A framework F intended to reason about an AGI must, at minimum, be capable of expressing the structure of the natural numbers (since recursive architectures implement discrete, iterated computation). This requirement is formalised as PA ⊆ F , where PA denotes Peano Arithmetic. The designer faces a strict dichotomy: 4• First-Order Logic (FO). By Gödel’s Completeness Theorem (1929), FO admits a sound and complete proof calculus: every semantically valid sentence is provable. However, by the Löwenheim–Skolem Theorem, FO cannot uniquely characterise the structure N — any FO theory with an infinite model has unintended, non-standard models. Consequently, FO is too weak to adequately constrain the reasoning about a recursive AGI and does not contain PA .

• Second-Order Logic (SO). SO allows quantification over sets and relations, and its standard semantics admits a categorical axiomatisation of N, closing the gap left by FO. However, SO has no sound and complete proof calculus (a consequence of Gödel’s Incompleteness, since SO can express arithmetic). Gaining descriptive power entails losing syntactic completeness. The conclusion is that any framework F expressive enough to model a Turing-complete AGI must satisfy PA ⊆ F , which places F squarely in the scope of Gödel’s theorems. 

Theorem 3 (Gödel Incompleteness, 1931) . Let F be a consistent, effectively generated formal system such that PA ⊆ F . Then F is incomplete: there exist sentences φ such that F ⊬ φ and 

F ⊬ ¬φ. Moreover, the consistency of F cannot be proved within F .Proof sketch (Diagonalisation Lemma). Since F contains PA , it can represent its own provability relation: there exists a formula Prov F (x) that holds (within F ) if and only if x is the Gödel number of a provable sentence. By the Diagonalisation Lemma, there exists a sentence G such that: 

F ⊢ G ↔ ¬ Prov F (⟨G⟩)

(G asserts its own unprovability.) We show G is undecidable by F :

• F ⊬ G: Suppose for contradiction that F ⊢ G. Then Prov F (⟨G⟩) holds (there is a proof of 

G). By the biconditional, F ⊢ ¬ Prov F (⟨G⟩), so F proves both Prov F (⟨G⟩) and its negation, contradicting consistency. 

• F ⊬ ¬G: Suppose F ⊢ ¬ G. Then F ⊢ Prov F (⟨G⟩) (from the biconditional), asserting that 

G has a proof. But if F is ω-consistent (the standard strengthening used by Gödel) or if one works with the Rosser variant, this again yields a contradiction. Hence F ⊬ ¬G.Thus neither G nor ¬G is provable in F : F is incomplete. 

Consequence for static alignment verification. Any framework F adequate for specifying and reasoning about a Turing-complete AGI contains PA , and is therefore subject to Gödel’s theorem. This means there exist alignment properties that are semantically true (the AGI is genuinely safe) yet unprovable within F . A static verifier operating in F will therefore either be incomplete (missing genuine safety guarantees) or inconsistent (producing false certificates). As Hernández-Espinosa et al. (2026) note, the system resides in a formal zone where the framework cannot determine the safe decision.

7.1 Exhaustiveness of the Two Vectors: A Formal Argument 

The dichotomy. Any algorithmic verification strategy for a safety property P of an AGI Mx oper-ates with respect to some domain of evaluation D. There are exactly two cases, jointly exhaustive and mutually exclusive: 1. Case 1 — Unbounded domain ( D infinite): The verifier evaluates P with respect to an infinite or unbounded domain — all possible inputs, all execution traces, or all models of an open first-order theory. This is Vector 1 (Infinite Means). 2. Case 2 — Bounded domain ( D finite): The verifier restricts evaluation to a finite universe — a fixed hardware description, a bounded model, or a finite test set. Its most ambitious form quantifies universally over all finite domains, seeking a universal finite certificate. This is Vector 2 (Finite Means). These cases are jointly exhaustive: any domain D is either finite or infinite, with no third option. Intermediate-looking strategies reduce to one of the two: 

• A verifier restricted to a fixed finite domain falls under Case 2; its certificate does not generalise beyond that structure. 

• A verifier quantifying over a parameterised family of finite domains (e.g., all configurations up to size n, for all n ∈ N) is equivalent to quantification over all finite structures — precisely Vector 2, governed by Trakhtenbrot’s Theorem. 

• A verifier using probabilistic methods over infinite input spaces remains within Vector 1; it is blocked by Rice’s Theorem at the level of exact guarantees.

# 8 Descriptive Complexity and Computational Barriers 

This gap between Alignment and Control can be understood by definitively linking the logical language with theoretical computational difficulty. Descriptive Complexity, founded by Fagin and Immerman, formally proves that the language required to describe a problem mathematically dic-tates the difficulty of verifying it, creating an inescapable structural correspondence over ordered finite structures: 

• First-Order Logic (FO): Too weak to express transitive closure or basic reachability in graphs, rendering it insufficient for modeling dynamic safety properties. 

• Least Fixed-Point Logic (LFP): Adds simple monotone recursion to the language. Ac-cording to the Immerman–Vardi theorem, over ordered finite structures, this logic corresponds exactly to the complexity class P (computationally tractable). 

• Second-Order Logic (SO): Adds the power to quantify over entire relations and sets, en-abling structural “guessing”. By Fagin’s Theorem, this corresponds exactly to the class N P 

(intractable to compute but efficiently verifiable). 

• Partial Fixed-Point Logic (PFP): Endows the system with deep, unrestricted non-monoto-ne recursion. Assuming ordered finite structures, it corresponds exactly to the class P SP ACE ,where memory and resolution time skyrocket toward absolute intractability. To model, specify, and audit the global and emergent behavior of a recursively self-improving AGI, a verification framework cannot settle for Least Fixed-Point logic (LFP). General alignment specifications inherently require the expressive power of Partial Fixed-Point logic (PFP) or higher to capture unbounded recursive dependencies. By the Immerman–Vardi and Vardi–Abiteboul the-orems, this jump in logical expressivity automatically transports the verification task from the tractable complexity class P into P SP ACE or worse. Consequently, the monitor’s task transi-tions from an efficient polynomial-time audit to a problem of intractable combinatorial explosion, rendering absolute safety guarantees computationally unreachable. 

Intractability in Finitude 

The perfect analogy to illustrate this condemnation is found on an 8 × 8 chessboard. Chess avoids Gödel’s problem of the mathematical infinite: the game tree and its states are strictly finite ( ∼ 10 44 

8different positions). Using Backward Induction from the final states to the beginning, the game is Decidable: an absolutely correct response always exists for every position. However, the combinatorial magnitude of this finite tree turns the evaluation into an Intractable problem (the generalized resolution of chess on N × N boards belongs precisely to the P SP ACE 

class). We can describe the problem (the “blueprint” is perfect), and the space is not infinite (the world is material and bounded), yet still, no monitor in the universe possesses the computational capacity to exhaust these states before the heat death of the universe. In fact, the hope of find-ing an efficient algorithm to quickly supervise this is equivalent to solving theoretical computer science’s greatest open mystery: determining if P = P SP ACE . Until this equivalence occurs, full expressivity inevitably equals blind intractability. 

# 9 Theorem of Finite Structural Unverifiability 

This Theorem refines the finite-means conclusion of Theorem 1 (via Theorem 4) by further stratifying the bounded-domain case into a tractability barrier (Clause 1) and a decidability barrier (Clause 2). 

Theorem 6 (Finite Structural Unverifiability of AGI Alignment) . Let M be a recursively self-improving agent architecture whose runtime code-modification dynamics require the expressive power of Partial Fixed-Point logic (PFP). Let ϕ ∈ PFP be a global alignment specification, and let F be the class of all ordered finite models representing hardware and operational environments. No verification framework V can exist that simultaneously satisfies Soundness (S), Completeness (C), and Tractability (T). The computational boundaries preventing the existence of such a verifier are structurally partitioned into two insurmountable mathematical limits: 1. The Local Boundary (Fixed Environments): In the worst case over alignment specifica-tions expressible in PFP , the decision problem of determining whether the agent satisfies the property over a fixed structure A ∈ F is strictly P SP ACE -hard under descriptive complexity. That is, there exist alignment specifications ϕ′ ∈ PFP (possibly distinct from the fixed ϕ above) for which no sub-exponential verification procedure can exist, assuming P̸ = P SP ACE .2. The Universal Boundary (Trakhtenbrot’s Wall): The universal finite verification prob-lem, defined as certifying safety across the entire class of all possible finite environment and hardware configurations: 

UnivVerify( M, ϕ ) ⇐⇒ ∀ A ∈ F (M, A ) |= ϕ

is strictly co-RE-complete. Thus, guaranteeing universal safety over bounded domains is algo-rithmically uncomputable. 

Remark. Note that the formulation of Clause 1 does not imply that every arbitrary alignment specification ϕ ∈ PFP is intractable. Rather, it guarantees the existence of critical, worst-case specifications that bound any universal verification framework within P SP ACE -hard limits.

In summary, Theorem 6 establishes a hybrid computational barrier, structurally partitioning the alignment verification problem into distinct strata of local intractability and universal undecidabil-ity. Within any isolated, fixed finite environment, the monitoring task is technically decidable but fundamentally intractable, trapped within the cosmic resource constraints of the P SP ACE -hard complexity class where exact auditing demands an impossible combinatorial explosion of memory. 9Conversely, the moment a safety framework attempts to generalize across the entire class of all possible finite operational environments, the problem breaches Trakhtenbrot’s Wall, transitioning into pure, absolute undecidability as a co-RE-complete, algorithmically uncomputable relation. 

Remark (The Paradox of Difficulty and Self-Reference) . This constitutes the fundamental reason why an AGI is inherently dangerous. Why does an AGI mirror Peano Arithmetic (PA) rather than Real Closed Fields (RCF)? Because an AGI operates on discrete code—analogous to the struc-tural building blocks of PA—which enables it to execute Gödel coding upon itself. Recursive self-improvement is, in essence, pure self-reference: a program modifying its own source code. By virtue of this capacity for self-reference, the agent inevitably falls into the trap of incompleteness, rendering its final execution state fundamentally unpredictable to any external monitor. 

# 10 Applied Engineering 

Translating the descriptive complexity barriers to the fields of software engineering and AI safety, the contemporary state of the art captures this structural collapse as a definitive impossibility result. The specific formulation of Soundness, Completeness, Tractability follows Agarwal (2026). 

Corollary 3 (Structural Derivation of the Soundness–Completeness–Tractability Trilemma) . As a direct consequence of the two structural limits established in Theorem 6, the semantic Pareto frontier of alignment certification collapses. For any verification architecture V , the simultaneous intersec-tion of the three foundational safety criteria yields a logical absurdity, establishing the trilemma not as a statistical anomaly, but as a structural logical necessity: 

∀V, ¬ S(V ) ∧ C(V ) ∧ T (V )

Proof. Assume for the sake of contradiction that there exists a verification framework V that simul-taneously satisfies Soundness, Completeness, and Tractability ( S ∧ C ∧ T ). By definition, a framework that is both sound and complete ( S ∧C) constitutes a perfect decision procedure for the alignment specification relation. Furthermore, by the criterion of Tractability ( T ), this decision procedure must execute within deterministic polynomial time. Consequently, the existence of such a verifier implies that the verification problem belongs to the complexity class P :

• By Theorem 6 (Clause 1): The fixed architecture verification problem is strictly P SP ACE -hard in the worst case over PFP specifications. Because co-RE-complete sets are strictly undecidable ( co -RE \ R), they admit no halting decision procedure, completely violating the constraints of P (co -RE ⊆ P is false). In both cases, the assumption that S, C, and T hold simultaneously forces a logically impossible collapse of the computational complexity hierarchy. Practical engineering, blocked by Trakhtenbrot’s Wall and the impossibility to generalize, is forced to systematically sacrifice Completeness to obtain safety certificates in highly bounded, frozen environments. Such containment strategies apply specifically to AGI architectures — systems whose internal expressivity is Turing-complete — by artificially restricting their effective behavior to a decidable fragment, rather than altering their underlying computational class. This renunciation materializes in a taxonomy of containment architectures, which achieve operational certainty by restricting the system’s scope: 1. Logical Isolation (Shielding): Surrounds the agent with a rigid monitor formatted within a decidable logical fragment (Alshiekh et al., 2018; Seshia et al., 2022). However, if a restricted language is used to guarantee halting in finite time, the AGI loses agency over decisions that are semantically inexpressible by that specific language. 2. Inversion of Burden (Proof-Carrying Code): The AGI is required to attach a formal deductive proof of safety before acting. While the verifier remains fast and tractable, the AGI becomes unable to execute legitimate, safe actions whose underlying proof exceeds the allowed structural or computational complexity. 3. Temporal “Myopia” (Bounded Horizon Planning): Amputates the agent’s asymptotic vision by limiting optimization to k future steps. This restriction makes the decision tree decidable at the cost of strategic blindness to long-term consequences. 4. Physical Confinement (AI Boxing): Converts the agent into a closed “Oracle” isolated from physical actuators and external networks, artificially stifling the causal expressivity of the environment. 11 Remark (The Paradox of Weakness) . By forcing the deployment environment to utilize weak log-ics, short horizons, or physical boxes to maintain decidability, any surreptitious malicious behavior designed by the AGI within its Turing-complete inner space that evades these basic operators will remain entirely invisible to the supervisor. 

# 11 Conclusions 

The challenge of AI alignment requires establishing formal guarantees that subject an autonomous system to human ethical coordinates. As demonstrated throughout this article, the ability to forge this link depends entirely on the level of mathematical expressivity demanded from the architecture and, crucially, on the inescapable limits of its verification. Mathematical research (Rice; Gödel; de Melo et al., 2025; Hernández-Espinosa et al., 2026) dictates that absolute certification over full, unbounded intelligence is a formal impossibility, es-tablishing the structural unverifiability of unconstrained recursive agents. Engineering theory and practice (Seshia et al., 2022) accept this verdict by deploying a tactical taxonomy of containment— such as Shielding, Boxing, and Temporal “Myopia.” These applied tools inhabit the Pareto Frontier of the Trilemma, carving out distinct oases of decidability through the programmed sacrifice of the system’s generality and completeness. In doing so, developers must accept that bypassing unverifia-bility forces the autonomous agent to suffer either paralysis by conservatism or structural blindness when confronted with superior, unmodelled environments. Furthermore, as Trakhtenbrot’s Wall concludes and the combinatorial scaling of chess demon-strates, the hope of overcoming these limitations by relying exclusively on the material, limited grounding of physical hardware is fallacious. Even if one accepts the claim by de Melo et al. (2025) that all machines halt and operate exclusively over finite domains, alignment safety remains struc-turally unverifiable—being universally incomputable by virtue of Trakhtenbrot’s Wall, and locally intractable under PSPACE constraints. While physical fixation allows for the ephemeral “particular safety” of a bounded or decidable testing environment, algorithmic intractability or outright incom-putability emerges completely intact the exact moment one attempts to scale this local certificate into a universal safety guarantee. This taxonomy establishes an inescapable triad of containment failures: an open or infinite domain yields fundamental undecidability (Rice and Gödel); a universal finite domain collapses into algorithmic incomputability via Trakhtenbrot’s theorem; and a particular finite domain traps the supervisor within intractable bounds. The sacrifice of expressivity is not a temporary software limitation to be overcome by engineering optimization, but the immutable toll that the laws of computation demand to grant us even the temporary illusion of control. Ultimately, our foundational mathematical journey proves that the core barrier to safety is not the impossibility of an aligned state, but its fundamental unverifiability. Because any robust, meaningful paradigm for AGI safety cannot be restricted to a single, statically frozen architecture but must certify containment universally, this universal boundary dictates that the overarching task of AGI alignment verification is fundamentally and inescapably undecidable. ■
